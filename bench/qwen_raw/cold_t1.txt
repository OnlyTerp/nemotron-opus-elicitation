Your understanding is close but incomplete. A closure is specifically a function that captures (closes over) variables from its enclosing scope, retaining access to those variables even after the outer function has returned. If the inner function doesn't reference any outer-scope variables, it's just a nested function — not a closure. The defining feature is the *capture* of free variables, not merely being defined inside another function.

Recursion and iteration are related but not the same. Iteration uses explicit control structures (for/while) and a single stack frame. Recursion calls the function on smaller subproblems, building up a call stack. Some problems (tree traversals, divide-and-conquer) are naturally recursive; others (simple repetition) are naturally iterative. Many can convert between each other, but they're conceptually different paradigms. Recursion also carries overhead (stack frames) and risks stack overflow for deep recursion. Choose based on problem structure, not assumed equivalence.

Two misconceptions here. Python lists are arrays of *pointers* to objects, not contiguous numeric data. Indexing is O(1) regardless of position — accessing the middle is just as fast as the end. So "always read from the end" won't optimize anything. For appends, regular lists already support O(1) amortized append. Use `collections.deque` if you need O(1) pops from the left. Don't rewrite your access patterns based on incorrect assumptions about list internals.
